# Litigation Activation: Higher-Order Analysis Through All Orders

## Context

The ratification counterfactual identified litigation (R7) as the primary enforcement mechanism for AI-era economic rights, scoring 18/25 at the second order when interacting with compliance theater (R-II). This document traces litigation activation through all higher orders to determine how courts would actually enforce AI-driven rights obligations.

This analysis operates in the post-OBBBA landscape: the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) slashed $990B from Medicaid, eliminated coverage for 10.9M Americans, raised taxes on the lowest earners while cutting them for the highest, and shortened Social Security/Medicare solvency timelines. The safety net has already sustained severe structural damage.

---

## Order 0: The Litigation Base

Legislation (whether ICESCR ratification or domestic statute) creates legally cognizable economic, social, and cultural rights with enforcement mechanisms. The question: which litigation mechanisms prove most effective?

---

## Order 1: Five Competing Litigation Mechanisms

### LA1: Individual Harm Claims
A person harmed by an AI system in a rights-critical domain sues for damages.
- Healthcare: AI misdiagnosis or treatment failure in Medicaid-contracted system
- Education: AI-driven educational platform fails to develop judgment capability
- Employment: AI hiring/firing system violates right to work
- Housing: AI tenant screening violates adequate living standard rights

### LA2: Class Action Structural Litigation
Groups challenge systemic AI-driven inequality.
- "AI-driven Medicaid eligibility determinations systematically exclude eligible recipients"
- "AI-powered education platforms fail to serve students with disabilities"
- "AI hiring systems produce discriminatory outcomes across protected classes"

### LA3: Qui Tam / Whistleblower Actions
Insiders at AI companies or deploying institutions expose quality failures.
- "This AI healthcare system doesn't meet the quality floor but received certification anyway"
- Modeled on False Claims Act: whistleblower shares in recovered funds
- Creates financial incentive for internal accountability

### LA4: State Attorney General Enforcement
State AGs bring cases representing their state populations.
- Modeled on tobacco litigation (1990s), opioid litigation (2010s-2020s), tech antitrust (2020s)
- AGs can coordinate multi-state actions
- Settlements create enforceable obligations + funding

### LA5: Regulatory Enforcement with Judicial Review
Administrative agencies enforce quality standards; courts adjudicate appeals.
- Modeled on FDA enforcement, EPA enforcement
- Agency expertise + judicial oversight
- Requires institutional capacity that may not exist post-OBBBA

### Scoring Reasoning

**LA4 (State AG Enforcement) dominates at 20/25.** The empirical record provides overwhelming support:
- **Tobacco**: 46-state coordinated litigation → $206B Master Settlement Agreement (1998)
- **Opioids**: multi-state litigation → $26B settlement with major distributors (2022)
- **Tech**: state AG antitrust actions against Google, Meta (2020s)
- Each case: AGs acted when federal government would not or could not

State AG litigation possesses structural advantages no other mechanism matches:
1. AGs have standing without individual harm (parens patriae doctrine)
2. AGs coordinate across states for maximum leverage
3. Settlements create enforceable obligations without legislation
4. Settlement funds flow to affected populations
5. AGs act independently of federal political dynamics

**LA3 (Qui Tam) scores 17** — the False Claims Act framework has proven effective for healthcare fraud and defense contractor accountability. Extending it to AI quality failures follows established patterns.

**LA1, LA2, LA5 all score 15** — functional but secondary mechanisms. Individual and class actions require individual plaintiffs who may lack resources. Regulatory enforcement requires institutional capacity that OBBBA degraded.

**Order 1 Survivors: LA4 (State AG) as dominant mechanism; LA3 (Qui Tam) as complementary.**

## Order 2: How State AG Litigation Develops

### LA4-1: Coordinated Multi-State Action
Multiple AGs bring coordinated cases against major AI companies deploying substandard systems in rights-critical domains. The coordination amplifies leverage and ensures consistent terms.

### LA4-2: Individual State Pioneering
One progressive state AG brings a test case. California, New York, or Massachusetts most likely — states with: strong AG offices, significant AI industry presence, progressive political alignment, and large affected populations.

### LA4-3: Federal-State Conflict
Federal government (potentially OBBBA-aligned administration) opposes state AG actions. AGs invoke state consumer protection authority and parens patriae standing. Federalism tensions produce complex legal battles.

### LA4-4: Settlement-Driven Policy
Litigation threat drives pre-litigation settlements. Companies agree to quality standards and access requirements to avoid the risk and cost of coordinated state action. Policy emerges from settlement terms rather than legislation.

## Order 3: The AI Master Settlement — Structure and Effects

### What the Settlement Contains

Drawing from tobacco, opioid, and tech antitrust settlement patterns, an AI Master Settlement would likely include:

**Quality Obligations**
- Minimum quality standards for AI systems in healthcare, education, employment
- Annual third-party audits of rights-critical AI systems
- Mandatory error reporting and incident disclosure

**Access Requirements**
- Free or reduced-cost access to AI tools for qualifying populations
- Non-discrimination requirements in AI-powered service delivery
- Accessibility standards (disability, language, digital literacy)

**Financial Terms**
- Ongoing annual payments from settling companies (scaled to AI revenue)
- Payments directed to state-administered funds for:
  - Safety net restoration (filling OBBBA gaps)
  - AI literacy and judgment development programs
  - Healthcare quality assurance
  - Transition support for AI-displaced workers

**Transparency**
- Algorithmic transparency requirements for rights-critical systems
- Public reporting on AI system performance in rights domains
- Open data requirements for monitoring effectiveness

**Institutional Structure**
- Multi-state monitoring body (modeled on NAAG tobacco protocol)
- Annual compliance review
- Escalation mechanism for non-compliance

**Quality standards (20/25) and institutional monitoring (18/25) survive as the strongest outcomes.** This matches tobacco settlement history — the most durable effects came from ongoing structural requirements, not one-time payments.

The countervailing hypothesis (industry adapts/evades, 16/25) represents a real risk. Tobacco companies adapted to settlement terms through product reformulation and market shifting. AI companies would likely restructure to minimize settlement obligations. This dynamic requires ongoing monitoring to counter.

### The Environmental Law Parallel

Environmental law provides the strongest analogy for how AI rights law could develop:

| Environmental Law (1970s-2000s) | AI Rights Law (projected) |
|---|---|
| Silent Spring (1962) → public awareness | AI productivity studies (2025-26) → public awareness |
| State-level action first (California emissions) | State-level AI quality action first |
| Federal framework (NEPA, Clean Air Act) | Progressive omnibus (if passed) or settlement terms |
| Private right of action + citizen suits | Private right of action + qui tam |
| EPA as institutional anchor | AI monitoring body as institutional anchor |
| Decades of case law defining standards | Decades of case law defining AI quality standards |
| Industry adaptation + evasion + compliance | Same dynamic expected |
| Genuine but incomplete environmental improvement | Genuine but incomplete rights improvement |
