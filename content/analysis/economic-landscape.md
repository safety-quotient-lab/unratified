# Current Economic and Geopolitical Landscape (March 2026)

## Overview

The global economy in early 2026 operates under compounding pressures: trade fragmentation, active military conflict, AI-driven transformation, and eroding multilateral cooperation. The World Economic Forum characterizes this moment as an "age of competition" where geoeconomic confrontation ranks as the #1 global risk.

This landscape directly shapes how AI's economic effects propagate — and who experiences the benefits or harms.

---

## Trade War and Tariff Regime

### Current Status (as of February 2026)

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that IEEPA does not authorize presidential tariff imposition. The administration responded by imposing tariffs under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974:
- **Flat 10-15% tariff** on all imports
- **50%+ additional tariff** on Chinese imports (effective rate exceeding 60%)
- China retaliated with 84% tariffs on U.S. imports

### Economic Impact Estimates
- GDP reduction: 1.3% long-run (before foreign retaliation)
- Employment: 550,000 fewer payroll jobs by end of 2026
- Inflation: 0.5 percentage point increase in 2025, additional 0.3pp in first half of 2026
- Average household cost: $1,500 annual tax increase equivalent
- Share of G20 imports covered by tariffs: largest increase in WTO monitoring history

### Relevance to AI Analysis
Tariffs create inflationary pressure on physical goods. AI-driven software productivity creates deflationary pressure on digital services. These forces operate in tension, with the net effect depending on sector and geography. Crucially, **AI-generated software cannot easily face tariffs** — digital goods route around trade barriers, making AI capability a partial offset to physical trade restrictions.

## Active Conflicts

### Ukraine-Russia War (Year 4)
- Total reconstruction cost estimate: $588 billion (nearly 3x Ukraine's projected nominal GDP)
- Average daily cost of full-scale war: $172 million (2025)
- Ukraine GDP growth: ~2% despite 8-14 hours daily power outages
- Russia sliding toward stagnation: growth near 1% long-term potential rate, VAT increased to 22%
- Russia lost its largest gas export market (Europe): from 150 bcm annually to 38 bcm

### Strategic Implications
Russia's restricted access to frontier AI models compounds its economic strain. Nations cut off from AI capability face the bifurcation effect (H7) at the state level, not just the firm level. Defense software development accelerates for AI-capable nations, widening military-technological gaps.

## AI Investment Landscape

### Capital Flows
- AI capital expenditure consensus for 2026: $527 billion (up from $465B estimate at start of Q3 2025)
- Global AI spending: ~$2 trillion in 2026, projected $3.3 trillion by 2029 (22% CAGR)
- Global IT spend exceeds $6 trillion in 2026

### Adoption Reality
- 34% of organizations deeply transform around AI (Deloitte)
- 30% redesign key processes
- 37% surface-level adoption only
- 75% of engineers use AI tools; most organizations see no measurable performance gains
- Only 19% report ROI boost exceeding 5%

### Relevance to Analysis
The gap between investment ($527B capex) and measured returns (75% no gains) suggests we observe constraint removal (H2) and Jevons expansion (H3) in the investment phase, with organizational productivity gains (H1) lagging. The massive capital commitment creates sunk-cost momentum — organizations push toward adoption regardless of current measured ROI.

## Supply Chain Reconfiguration

Governments globally pursue:
- **Friend-shoring**: redirecting supply chains to allied nations
- **Near-shoring**: moving production closer to end markets
- **De-risking**: reducing dependence on single-source suppliers (especially China)
- **Export controls**: restricting access to strategic technologies (AI chips, models)

Each reconfiguration requires extensive software infrastructure — logistics, compliance, monitoring, adaptation. This creates precisely the kind of new software demand that the constraint-removal model (H2) predicts AI would unlock.

## Connection to ICESCR Rights

| Current Condition | ICESCR Right Affected | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Tariff-driven inflation ($1,500/household) | Art. 11: Adequate standard of living | Direct cost-of-living increase |
| 550,000 fewer jobs projected | Art. 6: Right to work | Employment reduction |
| Trade fragmentation | Art. 1: Self-determination | Nations constrained in economic policy choices |
| AI investment/adoption gap | Art. 15: Right to science | Uneven access to AI benefits |
| War economy costs | Art. 9: Social security | Resources diverted from social programs |
| Energy demand from AI compute | Art. 11: Adequate standard of living | Energy cost and availability effects |

The compounding nature of these pressures — trade disruption + conflict costs + AI bifurcation + supply chain reconfiguration — creates conditions where economic, social, and cultural rights face simultaneous threats from multiple directions. A binding legal framework (ICESCR ratification) would provide structural response mechanisms. Without ratification, each pressure gets addressed ad hoc or not at all.
